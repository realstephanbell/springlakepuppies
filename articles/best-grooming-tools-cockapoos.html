Why Cockapoo Grooming Is Different

Most generic dog brushes are designed for straight or single-layer coats. Cockapoos have a dense, wavy-to-curly double coat that traps loose hair instead of shedding it. That trapped hair is what causes mats — usually behind the ears, under the collar, and in the armpits.

The tools below are chosen specifically to address this. You need something that penetrates the coat, not just skims the surface.

Pro tip: Always brush before bathing. Wet mats tighten and become nearly impossible to remove without cutting. A 5-minute dry brush session before the tub saves you 30 minutes of frustration afterward.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I brush my Cockapoo?

Ideally 3–4 times per week for wavy coats, and daily for tighter curly coats. The areas behind the ears, under the collar, and in the armpits are mat hotspots — check those every time.

When should I take my Cockapoo to a professional groomer?

Every 6–8 weeks is standard for a full groom and cut. Home maintenance between appointments keeps the coat from matting and makes professional appointments faster and less expensive.

What do I do if my Cockapoo is already matted?

Use a detangler spray, work from the ends of the mat inward (not the root), and use your fingers to break it apart before introducing a brush. For severe matting, a professional groomer is worth the cost.

Is the Chris Christensen brush really worth the price?

For Cockapoos, yes. The quality difference is immediately noticeable on dense, curly-wavy coats. If you're brushing 3–4 times a week, having the right tool makes that routine something your dog actually tolerates.

The Bottom Line

Here's the simplest way that works for Cockapoo grooming: a quality slicker brush, a detangler spray, and patience in the early months while your puppy gets used to the routine. Add clippers once you're confident enough to handle face and paw trims at home. That's it.
